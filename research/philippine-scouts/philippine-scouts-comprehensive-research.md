## 1. Philippine Scouts Unit History (1901-1945)

### Formation and Early Years (1901-1920)

The Philippine Scouts were a military organization of the United States Army from 1901 until after the end of World War II.[^1]

**Key Formation Dates:**
- **September 10, 1899:** The Macabebe Scouts were formed under Lieutenant Matthew A. Batson as the precursor organization
- **October 1901:** President Theodore Roosevelt authorized the formation of a unified native Scouts organization
- **1901:** Army Reorganization Act authorized the formation of scout units of Philippine origin within the U.S. Army
- **5,000 men:** Initial force integrated from 50 local companies

**Macabebe Scouts Background:**
The Macabebes, from the municipality of Macabebe in Pampanga, had a history of service in the Spanish Army militia. They joined U.S. forces under General Henry Lawton and later General Elwell Otis. On June 26, 1898, in retaliation for their alliance with American forces, Aguinaldo's forces under Colonel Agapito Bonzon burned the town of Macabebe and massacred 300 residents. The Macabebes won lasting notability in the operation under General Frederick Funston to capture General Aguinaldo.[^2]

### Organizational Structure (1919-1941)

**Regiment Formation (1919-1920):**
In 1919-1920, Philippine Scout companies were grouped into formal regiments:

**Infantry Regiments:**
- 43rd Infantry Regiment (PS)
- 44th Infantry Regiment  
- 45th Infantry Regiment (PS)
- 57th Infantry Regiment (PS)

**Artillery Regiments:**
- 24th Field Artillery Regiment (PS)
- 25th Field Artillery Regiment (PS)

**Cavalry:**
- 26th Cavalry Regiment (PS)

**Coast Artillery:**
- 91st Coast Artillery Regiment
- 92nd Coast Artillery Regiment

**Support Units:**
- Engineer battalions
- Medical units
- Quartermaster units
- Military police units

These regiments, together with the U.S. 31st Infantry Regiment, formed the U.S. Army's Philippine Division. At this point, the Scouts became the U.S. Army's front line troops in the Pacific.[^1]

### Filipino Officers

**West Point Program:**
In 1910, the U.S. Army began sending one outstanding Filipino soldier per year to the United States Military Academy at West Point. Notable graduates included:

- **Vicente Lim** (USMA Class of 1914) - First Filipino graduate of West Point
- **Fidel V. Segundo** (1917)
- **Eustaquio S. Baclig** (1918)
- **Pastor Martelino** (1920)
- At least eight others received commissions from West Point

**First Filipino Officer:**
Esteban Boadilla Dalao was the first Filipino officer commissioned with the Philippine Scouts in 1912, rising to the rank of Colonel.[^3]

**By July 1941:** There were fifteen Filipino Scout officers within the Philippine Division distributed as follows:
- 2 in Headquarters
- 2 amongst Special Troops
- 3 in 45th Infantry Regiment (PS)
- 1 in 57th Infantry Regiment (PS)
- 5 in 24th Field Artillery Regiment (PS)
- 1 in 12th Quartermaster Regiment (PS)
- 1 in 14th Engineer Regiment (PS)

### Strength and Composition (1941)

**Total Philippine Division Strength (July 31, 1941):**
- **Total Personnel:** 10,473
- **Officers:** 516 (including 15 Filipino Scout officers)
- **Enlisted:** 9,957
  - Americans: 2,036
  - Filipinos (Philippine Scouts): 7,921

**USAFFE Formation (July 26, 1941):**
When President Roosevelt called General Douglas MacArthur back to active duty and put him in charge of United States Army Forces in the Far East (USAFFE), the force consisted of:
- **Total Troops:** 22,532
- **Philippine Scouts:** 11,972
- **Philippine Division:** 10,473 (containing 2,552 Americans and 7,921 Filipino Scouts)

## 2. The Philippine Division and Carabao Insignia

### Division Organization

The Philippine Division was the U.S. Army's primary combat formation in the Philippines, combining American and Filipino Scout units.

**Key Components:**
- **31st Infantry Regiment (United States)** - All-American unit
- **45th Infantry Regiment (PS)** - Philippine Scouts
- **57th Infantry Regiment (PS)** - Philippine Scouts
- **26th Cavalry Regiment (PS)** - Philippine Scouts (horse-mounted cavalry)
- **24th Field Artillery Regiment (PS)**
- **Support and service units**

### The Carabao (Water Buffalo) Insignia

**Symbolism:**
The Philippine Scouts shoulder sleeve insignia from 1921-1947 featured the head of a **carabao** (water buffalo), which symbolized the Philippines.[^1]

**Design Elements:**
- **Colors:** Red and gold, representing the islands' Spanish colonial roots
- **Central Image:** Carabao head, a symbol deeply connected to Filipino agriculture and culture
- **Period of Use:** 1921-1947

The carabao was chosen as it represented:
- The agricultural heritage of the Philippines
- Strength and endurance
- Filipino identity and cultural pride

This insignia distinguished the Philippine Division and Philippine Scout units from other U.S. Army formations.

## 3. Role in Defending Bataan (January-April 1942)

### Strategic Situation

**War Plan Orange 3 (WPO-3):**
The pre-war defense plan called for:
- Holding the entrance to Manila Bay
- Withdrawing to the Bataan Peninsula if necessary
- Defending Bataan to the "last extremity"
- Bataan was recognized as key to control of Manila Bay

**MacArthur's Initial Plan:**
General MacArthur rejected WPO-3 as defeatist and preferred a coastal defense strategy covering the entire archipelago. However, after Japanese landings, he was forced to revert to WPO-3.[^4]

### Defensive Lines on Bataan

**Retreat to Bataan (December 24, 1941 - January 7, 1942):**

Philippine Scouts and other USAFFE forces conducted a fighting withdrawal through several defensive lines:

**D1-D5 Lines (Northern Luzon):**
- D1: Aguilar to San Carlos to Urdaneta City
- D2: Agno River
- D3: Santa Ignacia to Gerona to Guimba to San Jose
- D4: Tarlac to Cabanatuan
- D5: Bamban to Sibul Springs

**Entry into Bataan:**
- January 5-6, 1942: The 21st and 11th Divisions crossed into Bataan
- The defenders successfully withdrew approximately 80,000 troops (15,000 Americans and 65,000 Filipinos) to Bataan

### The Main Battle Lines

**Abucay-Mauban Line (January 7-23, 1942):**

**I Philippine Corps (West - Mauban Line):**
- Commander: Major General Jonathan Wainwright
- Strength: 22,500 troops
- Units:
  - 1st Regular Division (Gen. Fidel Segundo)
  - 31st Division (BG Clifford Bluemel)
  - 91st Division (BG Luther R. Stevens)
  - 26th Cavalry (PS)

**II Philippine Corps (East - Abucay Line):**
- Commander: Major General George Parker
- Strength: 25,000 troops
- Units:
  - 11th Division
  - 21st Division
  - 41st Division (BG Vicente Lim)
  - 51st Division (BG Albert M. Jones)
  - 57th Infantry (PS) (Colonel George S. Clarke)

**Gap in the Line:**
Mount Natib (4,222 feet/1,287 m) and Mount Silanganan (3,620 feet/1,100 m) separated the two corps. Commanders considered this rugged terrain impassable, creating a serious weakness that the Japanese would exploit.

### Philippine Scouts in Combat

**Battle of Abucay (January 9-22, 1942):**

The Japanese 65th Brigade under Akira Nara attacked the Abucay Line with approximately 23,222 soldiers against 15,000 Americans and 65,000 Filipinos.

**57th Infantry Regiment (PS) Actions:**
- Bore the brunt of initial Japanese attacks starting January 9
- Engaged in fierce fighting at Abucay Hacienda
- By January 12, required reinforcement from the 21st Infantry
- Scene of 2nd Lieutenant Alexander R. Nininger's Medal of Honor action (January 12)
- Continued fighting until January 22 when withdrawal was ordered

**Philippine Division Response:**
On January 14, the elite Philippine Division was committed to the battle:
- 45th Infantry (PS)
- 31st Infantry (US)
- These units joined the counterattacks between January 19-22

**Medal of Honor Actions:**

1. **Sergeant Jose Calugas** (45th Infantry, PS)
   - Action: Culis, Bataan, January 6, 1942
   - First Medal of Honor awarded in WWII to a Philippine Scout

2. **2nd Lieutenant Alexander R. Nininger** (57th Infantry, PS)  
   - Action: Near Abucay, Bataan, January 12, 1942
   - Armed only with rifle and hand grenades, forced his way into enemy foxholes during hand-to-hand combat
   - Permitted his unit to retake Abucay Hacienda
   - Posthumously awarded Medal of Honor - first Army Medal of Honor in WWII

3. **1st Lieutenant Willibald C. Bianchi** (45th Infantry, PS)
   - Action: Near Bagac, Bataan, February 3, 1942
   - Led reinforced platoon against two enemy machine-gun nests
   - Silenced them with grenades despite two gunshot wounds to chest
   - Manned antiaircraft machine gun until knocked off tank by third wound
   - Posthumously awarded Medal of Honor

**Battle of the Points (January 22 - February 13, 1942):**

When Japanese forces landed on the western coast attempting to outflank I Corps, Philippine Scout units were crucial in eliminating these threats:

- **45th Infantry (PS):** Deployed against Japanese landings at Quinauan Point and Anyasan-Silaiim
- **57th Infantry (PS):** Elements helped contain and eliminate the landing forces
- **24th Field Artillery (PS):** Provided fire support

The battle lasted from January 22 to February 13, resulting in complete destruction of Japanese 1st and 2nd Battalions, 20th Infantry.

### Orion-Bagac Line (January 26 - April 3, 1942)

After withdrawal from the Abucay-Mauban Line, the defenders established a new defensive line:

**Final Defensive Positions:**
- Length: Approximately 20 miles across Bataan Peninsula
- Eastern terminus: Orion (Manila Bay side)
- Western terminus: Bagac (South China Sea side)

**Philippine Scouts Role:**
- Formed the backbone of the defensive line
- Described by survivors as "the backbone of the American defense"
- Better trained and equipped than Philippine Army units
- Maintained discipline despite:
  - Reduced to half-rations in January 1942
  - Ravaged by malaria, dysentery, and malnutrition
  - No reinforcements or adequate supplies

**Conditions:**
- Food stocks insufficient - reduced to half rations January 1942
- Malaria, dengue fever, and dysentery prevalent
- No adequate medical supplies
- Constant Japanese air and artillery bombardment
- Promised reinforcements never arrived

## 4. Battle Tactics During the Bataan Campaign

### Defensive Tactics

**Delaying Actions:**
The Philippine Scouts excelled at conducting fighting withdrawals through multiple defensive lines, allowing the bulk of forces to reach Bataan safely.

**Key Tactical Elements:**

1. **Prepared Defensive Positions**
   - Construction of field fortifications
   - Use of natural terrain (Mount Natib, Mount Silanganan)
   - Establishing kill zones covering approaches

2. **Artillery Support**
   - 24th Field Artillery Regiment (PS) provided crucial fire support
   - According to Japanese sources: "Enemy artillery was so accurate and powerful that the Japanese Army feared this most"[^5]
   - Coordination between infantry and artillery units

3. **Cavalry Operations**
   - 26th Cavalry Regiment (PS) conducted:
     - Reconnaissance missions
     - Delaying actions
     - The **last horse cavalry charge in U.S. military history** (led by 1st Lieutenant Edwin Ramsey)
     - Screening and covering force missions

4. **Combined Arms Coordination**
   - Infantry-artillery cooperation
   - Tank support from 192nd and 194th Tank Battalions
   - Integration of American and Filipino Scout units

### Offensive Tactics

**Counterattacks:**

1. **January 16-22, 1942: Abucay Counteroffensive**
   - 51st Division ordered to counterattack
   - Met head-on by Japanese 141st and 9th Infantry
   - Philippine Division (45th and 31st Infantry) committed
   - Limited success but prevented Japanese breakthrough

2. **February 2-8, 1942: Trail 2 Counterattacks**
   - 41st Infantry, 51st Combat Team, 31st Engineer Battalion
   - Supported by 21st Division and 32nd Infantry
   - Forced Japanese 65th Brigade to withdraw

**Small Unit Tactics:**

Based on veteran accounts, Philippine Scouts employed:
- **Patrol actions** to gather intelligence
- **Ambush tactics** against Japanese infiltration attempts  
- **Close combat proficiency** with rifles, machine guns, and hand grenades
- **Night operations** to counter Japanese night infiltration tactics

### Jungle Warfare Adaptations

1. **Terrain Utilization**
   - Use of dense jungle for concealment
   - Establishment of observation posts on high ground
   - Creation of cleared fields of fire

2. **Supply Line Defense**
   - Protection of the few roads through Bataan
   - Defense of supply dumps and water sources

3. **Anti-infiltration Measures**
   - Constant patrols behind defensive lines
   - Elimination of Japanese "pockets" that penetrated defenses

### Challenges Faced

**Tactical Disadvantages:**
- **Air Superiority:** Japanese controlled the air after destruction of Far East Air Force
- **Naval Blockade:** Prevented reinforcement and resupply
- **Numerical Inferiority:** Facing larger Japanese forces
- **Gap Between Corps:** Mount Natib area allowed Japanese penetration
- **Starvation Rations:** Reduced combat effectiveness
- **Disease:** Malaria, dysentery, and dengue fever decimated ranks

**According to survivors:** "The troops have been on half rations for three months and are now on less than that amount which results in much loss of physical vigor and sickness."[^6]

## 5. The Bataan Death March

### Overview

**Dates:** April 9-17, 1942  
**Distance:** Approximately 65 miles (105 km) total
- Foot march: ~42 km (average 14 km per day over 3 days)
- Rail transport: San Fernando to Capas

**Numbers:**
- **Prisoners:** 72,000-78,000 total
  - ~12,000 Americans
  - ~66,000 Filipinos
- **Deaths during march:** Estimates vary widely
  - American deaths: 500-650
  - Filipino deaths: 5,000-18,000

### Route and Conditions

**March Route:**
1. **Starting Points:**
   - Mariveles (southern tip of Bataan)
   - Bagac (western Bataan)
   
2. **Convergence:** Pilar

3. **Northern Route:** To San Fernando (Pampanga)

4. **Rail Transport:** Packed into boxcars (100 prisoners per car)
   - In 43°C (110°F) heat
   - No ventilation, sanitation, or water
   - One-hour trip to Capas

5. **Final March:** 9 miles (14 km) from Capas to Camp O'Donnell

### Atrocities and Treatment

**Japanese Treatment:**
- Forced to surrender all possessions
- Little to no food or water provided
- Severe physical abuse and beatings
- Summary executions of those who:
  - Fell behind
  - Asked for water
  - Were too weak to continue
- "Sun treatment" torture - forced to sit in direct sunlight without cover

**Specific Incidents:**

1. **Pantingan River Massacre**
   - 350-400 Filipino officers and NCOs summarily executed
   - Ordered by Colonel Masanobu Tsuji
   - Occurred after their surrender

2. **Possession Searches**
   - Japanese confiscated jewelry and valuables
   - Soldiers with Japanese souvenirs or money were executed
   - Some had fingers cut off to remove rings

3. **Vehicular Killings**
   - Trucks drove over prisoners who collapsed
   - "Cleanup crews" killed those too weak to continue

**Survivor Testimony - Staff Sergeant Alf Larson:**
> "They packed us in the cars like sardines, so tight you couldn't sit down... It was close to summer and the weather was hot and humid, hotter than Billy Blazes! We were on the train from early morning to late afternoon without getting out. People died in the railroad cars."[^7]

**Survivor Testimony - American Lieutenant Kermit Lay:**
> "They pulled us off into a rice paddy and began shaking us down... Everyone had pulled their pockets wrong side out and laid all their things out in front. They were taking jewelry and doing a lot of slapping... After the shakedown, the Japs took an officer and two enlisted men behind a rice shack and shot them."[^7]

### Casualty Estimates

**Conservative Estimate (Stanley L. Falk):**
Based on documented evidence:
- American deaths: 600-650
- Filipino deaths: 5,000-10,000

**Other Source Estimates:**
- American deaths: 500-650
- Filipino deaths: 5,000-18,000

**Factors Contributing to High Death Rate:**
- Starvation (forces already on half or quarter rations)
- Dehydration
- Disease (malaria, dysentery, dengue fever)
- Physical exhaustion from Battle of Bataan
- Japanese brutality and summary executions
- Lack of medical care
- Extreme heat

### War Crimes Prosecution

**General Masaharu Homma:**
- Arrested September 1945
- Charged with 43 counts of war crimes
- Found guilty of permitting subordinates to commit "brutal atrocities and other high crimes"
- Sentenced to death February 26, 1946
- Executed by firing squad April 3, 1946, outside Manila

**Other Officers Prosecuted:**
- **Major General Yoshitaka Kawane** - Death by hanging, June 12, 1949
- **Colonel Kurataro Hirano** - Death by hanging, June 12, 1949

**Colonel Masanobu Tsuji:**
- Directly ordered killing of POWs (Pantingan River Massacre)
- Fled to China and then Thailand
- Escaped prosecution

**Legal Basis:**
- Doctrine of *respondeat superior* (command responsibility)
- Failure to prevent subordinates from committing atrocities

## 6. POW Camps

### Camp O'Donnell

**Location:** Capas, Tarlac Province, Luzon  
**Original Purpose:** Philippine Army training camp designed for 10,000 men  
**POW Capacity:** 60,000 men crammed in

**Conditions:**
- Severely overcrowded (6x designed capacity)
- Little running water
- Sparse food rations
- No medical care
- Only slit trenches for sanitation
- Intolerable heat
- Flies from latrines covered prisoners' food

**Commander's Speech:**
The Japanese commander greeted new arrivals with the "Goddamn you to Hell" speech, stating that:
- Prisoners were "captives," not POWs
- Would not be treated according to Geneva Conventions
- Essentially had no rights

**Death Toll:**
- **Americans:** Approximately 1,500 died
- **Filipinos:** Approximately 26,000 died (out of 50,000)
- **Death Rate:** Up to 400 per day at peak
- **Operational Period:** April-September 1942 (8 months)
- **Diseases:** Malaria, dysentery, beriberi, and other diseases swept through the camp

**Release of Filipino Prisoners:**
From September through December 1942, the Japanese gradually paroled surviving Philippine Scouts and other Filipino soldiers to:
- Their families
- The mayors of their hometowns
- Many would later join guerrilla resistance

### Camp Cabanatuan

**Location:** Cabanatuan, Nueva Ecija Province, Luzon  
**Established:** June 1942  
**Type:** Main POW camp for American prisoners after Camp O'Donnell

**Conditions:**
- Marginally better than Camp O'Donnell
- Continued food shortages
- Inadequate medical care
- Disease remained rampant
- Forced labor details
- Continued deaths from malnutrition and disease

**Population:**
- Consolidated American POWs from Bataan and Corregidor
- Peak population: approximately 8,000-9,000 prisoners
- Many prisoners later transferred to Japan or other camps

**Notable Events:**
- **January 30, 1945:** Raid at Cabanatuan
  - U.S. Army Rangers and Filipino guerrillas
  - Freed 513 POWs
  - One of the most successful rescue operations in U.S. military history

### Hell Ships to Japan

**Purpose:** Transfer of POWs to Japan and Manchuria for slave labor

**Conditions on Hell Ships:**
- Prisoners crammed into cargo holds
  - So tight they could not sit or lay down
  - Some suffocated while standing
- Extreme heat in closed holds
- No food or water (minimal rations)
- No sanitary facilities
- No ventilation
- Ships were unmarked

**Casualties:**
- Many ships torpedoed by American submarines (unaware they carried POWs)
- Thousands died from:
  - Suffocation
  - Dehydration
  - Disease
  - Torpedoing of ships

**Notable Hell Ships:**
- **Oryoku Maru** (December 1944)
- **Enoura Maru** (January 1945)
- **Brazil Maru** (January 1945)
- Many others

### Work Camps in Japan

**Locations:**
- Various locations throughout Japan
- Manchuria (northeastern China)
- Korea

**Labor:**
- Mining operations
- Factory work
- Dock work
- Construction projects
- Often in dangerous conditions

**Conditions:**
- Continued malnutrition
- Harsh winter weather (many from Philippines unaccustomed to cold)
- Industrial accidents
- Continued deaths from disease and overwork
- Some camps more brutal than others

**Liberation:**
- August 1945 following Japanese surrender
- Some POWs not liberated until September 1945
- By liberation, two-thirds of American Bataan prisoners had died in Japanese custody

## 7. Guerrilla Resistance Activities by Escaped Philippine Scouts

### Formation of Guerrilla Units

**Timing and Circumstances:**
- Many Philippine Scouts and Filipino soldiers refused to surrender on April 9, 1942
- Fled into the mountains and jungles of Bataan and throughout the Philippines
- Approximately 10,000-12,000 escapees from the Death March
- "Paroled" Filipino soldiers (released from Camp O'Donnell in late 1942) joined resistance

**Motivation:**
- Unwillingness to accept defeat
- Desire to continue fighting
- Local knowledge of terrain
- Support from local civilian population

### Leadership

**American Officers:**
Some American officers who escaped provided leadership and liaison with Allied forces:

- **Colonel Wendell Fertig**
  - Led guerrilla forces in Mindanao
  - Promoted himself to Brigadier General
  - Organized extensive resistance network

- **Lieutenant Colonel Edwin Ramsey**
  - Former 26th Cavalry (PS) officer
  - Led guerrillas in Central Luzon
  - Had commanded the last U.S. cavalry charge at Morong

- **Major Ray C. Hunt**
  - Organized guerrilla operations
  - Later wrote memoir of experiences

**Filipino Scout Officers:**
Many Filipino Scout officers took leadership roles:
- Used their U.S. Army training
- Maintained military organization and discipline
- Coordinated with both American and Filipino guerrilla leaders

### Organization and Structure

**Guerrilla Units Incorporated Former Scouts:**

1. **Central Luzon:**
   - Hukbalahap (People's Anti-Japanese Army) - some Scout veterans
   - USAFFE guerrillas - many former Scouts under American officers
   - Squadron commanders who were former Scout NCOs and officers

2. **Mindanao:**
   - 10th Military District (Fertig's command)
   - Included many former Philippine Scout soldiers
   - Well-organized with military ranks and structure

3. **Visayas:**
   - Multiple guerrilla organizations
   - Scout veterans provided military expertise

4. **Bataan and Zambales:**
   - Local guerrilla groups formed by escapees
   - Intimate knowledge of terrain from the campaign

**Military Organization:**
- Maintained formal military ranks
- Organized into companies, battalions, regiments
- Established supply and communication networks
- Coordinated operations across regions

### Guerrilla Activities and Tactics

**Intelligence Gathering:**
- Monitored Japanese troop movements
- Reported to Allied submarines and later to MacArthur's headquarters
- Provided detailed information on Japanese installations and defenses
- Critical for planning the Philippine liberation

**Sabotage Operations:**
- Destroyed bridges and roads
- Attacked supply convoys
- Disrupted Japanese communications
- Destroyed military equipment and supplies

**Ambush and Raid Tactics:**
- Small-unit actions against Japanese patrols
- Attacks on isolated garrisons
- Hit-and-run operations
- Avoided large-scale engagements initially

**Protection of Civilians:**
- Defended Filipino communities from Japanese reprisals
- Assisted downed Allied airmen
- Helped escaped POWs reach safety

**Coast Watching:**
- Reported Japanese naval movements
- Assisted Allied submarines
- Monitored air traffic

### Support from Local Population

**Civilian Support Network:**
- Food and supplies provided by Filipino civilians
- Intelligence from civilian sources
- Shelter and hiding places
- Warning systems for Japanese movements

**Consequences for Civilians:**
- Japanese reprisals against communities supporting guerrillas
- Massacres in suspected guerrilla support areas
- Destruction of villages

### Contact with Allied Forces

**Submarine Operations:**
- U.S. submarines delivered:
  - Weapons and ammunition
  - Radio equipment
  - Medical supplies
  - Money and gold
- Evacuated important personnel
- Provided communication link to MacArthur's headquarters

**Radio Communications:**
- Establishment of radio networks
- Daily communication with Allied headquarters
- Coordination of activities
- Intelligence reporting

**Recognition:**
- MacArthur's headquarters officially recognized many guerrilla units
- Provided supplies and direction
- Integrated into liberation plans

### Effectiveness and Impact

**Strategic Value:**
- Tied down significant Japanese forces (estimated 50,000+ troops)
- Forces needed for garrison duty couldn't be used elsewhere
- Constant pressure on Japanese logistics and morale

**Intelligence Contribution:**
- Detailed mapping of Japanese positions
- Order of battle information
- Early warning of Japanese movements
- Critical for MacArthur's return planning

**Support for Liberation (1944-1945):**
- Guided advancing American forces
- Identified Japanese positions
- Protected infrastructure from Japanese demolition
- Prevented Japanese scorched-earth tactics in some areas
- Helped liberate POW camps

**Example - Cabanatuan Raid (January 30, 1945):**
- Filipino guerrillas (including former Scouts) provided:
  - Intelligence on camp layout and guards
  - Route planning
  - Blocking forces to prevent Japanese reinforcement
  - Security for the rescue operation
  - Guides for the rescued POWs

### Post-War Recognition

**Integration into Philippine Army:**
- Many guerrilla veterans joined the reformed Philippine military
- Experience provided foundation for post-war armed forces

**U.S. Recognition:**
- Some guerrilla units officially recognized by U.S. Army
- Veterans eligible for U.S. benefits (though many claims disputed)
- Medals and citations awarded

**Philippines Recognition:**
- Honored as resistance heroes
- Veterans' benefits in the Philippines
- Annual commemorations
